# Project 507 — CruX UCLA Neurotech Industry Outreach Campaign Plan

**Owner:** Brian Wu, Outreach Officer, CruX UCLA
**Duration:** 6 weeks
**Primary directory:** [Neurofounders Startup Map](https://www.neurofounders.co/resources/start-up-map)

## Campaign Goal

Establish contact and build long-term relationships with early-to-mid stage neurotech startups and Southern California research institutes by:

1. Introducing CruX UCLA as a premier hub for student neurotech talent.
2. Sharing the sponsorship/partnership prospectus once interest is confirmed.
3. Exploring collaborations (sponsorships or student consulting engagements) via low-friction, no-meeting CTAs.

---

## Phase 1: Target Discovery & Research (Weeks 1–2)

### Sub-goal 1.1 — Build the target list (Week 1)

- Filter the Neurofounders map by **Region: North America** and **Funding: Pre-seed through Series B**. Early-to-mid stage companies are most receptive: they lack established university pipelines and value inexpensive talent access.
- Prioritize categories where CruX has demonstrable credibility: **Brain-Computer Interface, Consumer Neurotech, Tools & Infrastructure**. The EEG neurofeedback project maps directly onto companies such as Alljoined, Arctop, BrainBit, Emotiv, Elemind, and Cognixion.
- Target **40–50 startups**, tiered:
  - **Tier A (10–15):** Strong technical overlap with CruX projects, US-based, ideally California.
  - **Tier B (15–20):** Relevant category, weaker geographic or technical fit.
  - **Tier C (remainder):** Exploratory contacts.
- Separately, list **8–12 research institutes**. Southern California candidates: UCLA Brain Research Institute, Semel Institute (UCLA), Chen Institute (Caltech), USC Neurorestoration Center, and neural engineering labs at UC Irvine and UCSD.

### Sub-goal 1.2 — Find contacts (Weeks 1–2)

- For startups under ~30 employees, email the founder or CEO directly; at that size, the founder decides on sponsorships.
- Sources, in order of efficiency: company website (About/Team pages) → LinkedIn → standard address patterns (first@company.com) verified with a free tool such as Hunter.io or a manual check.
- **Method note:** Do not scrape the map programmatically or mass-harvest emails. Manual research at this volume takes only a few hours per week, remains compliant with site terms and CAN-SPAM norms, and produces better personalization.

### Sub-goal 1.3 — Set up the tracker (Week 1)

One spreadsheet with columns: Company, Category, Tier, Contact Name, Role, Email, Personalization Hook, Date Sent, Bump 1 Date, Bump 2 Date, Status (Not Sent / Sent / Replied / Deck Sent / Warm / Closed), Notes, Handoff Owner.

---

## Phase 2: Outreach Execution (Weeks 2–5)

- **Volume:** 12–15 emails per week, sent Tuesday–Thursday mornings. Quality beats volume decisively in cold outreach.
- **Sequencing:** Send Tier A first, while energy and message quality are highest.
- **Cadence per contact:** Initial email → short bump at day 5 → final bump at day 12 → mark Closed at day 21 if silent. Never more than two bumps; each bump should add something small (a project link, a one-line update) rather than merely asking again.
- **Weekly review (Fridays, 30 min):** Update statuses, compute reply rate, revise the weakest-performing subject line or opening.

---

## Phase 3: Relationship Nurturing (Weeks 4–6 and ongoing)

- **Deck delivery:** Send the prospectus within 24 hours of any expression of interest, with a two-sentence email pointing to the single most relevant page.
- **Lead qualification:** A lead is Warm once the recipient asks about pricing, timelines, or team capabilities.
- **Handoff:** Introduce warm leads to the operations lead via a three-line email naming both parties, stating the topic, and proposing next steps. Brian stays copied to preserve relationship continuity.
- **Long-term nurture:** Add polite decliners and silent Tier A contacts to a quarterly touch list; send a brief update when CruX ships a project result.

---

## Success Metrics (KPIs)

| KPI | Target | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Emails sent | 45–60 total | Execution discipline |
| Reply rate | 8–15% | Healthy for cold student outreach; below 5% means the hook or targeting needs revision |
| Positive reply rate | 5–10% | Message and audience fit |
| Decks sent | 5–8 | Phase 3 pipeline health |
| Warm leads handed off | 2–4 | The true success measure |
| Bump conversion | Track separately | Bumps often generate half of all replies; validates the cadence |

Deck opens are only trackable with link tracking (e.g., DocSend or a Google Drive link with view analytics).

**Subject:** UCLA neurotech students × [Company Name]

> Hi [Contact Name],
>
> I came across [Company Name] on the Neurofounders Startup Map and was genuinely impressed by your work on [Specific Tech/Product]. Your approach to [specific niche, e.g., noninvasive EEG decoding / closed-loop neuromodulation] overlaps directly with projects our members build.
>
> I am Brian Wu, Outreach Officer at CruX UCLA. We are UCLA's neurotechnology organization run entirely by students, and our teams design and ship working BCI systems, including an EEG neurofeedback pipeline currently in development. We partner with companies through sponsorships and through student consulting teams that take on scoped technical or business problems.

> I am reaching out simply to share resources and see whether there is high-level alignment; I am not asking for a meeting. Would you be open to reviewing a one-page overview of how we partner with industry teams? If someone else at [Company Name] handles partnerships, I would be grateful for a pointer to the right person.

> Best regards,
> Brian Wu
> Outreach Officer, CruX UCLA
